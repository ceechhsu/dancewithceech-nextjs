## Dance History of The Farmer
The Farmer is a testament to Hip Hop's rich tradition of drawing from diverse cultural wells. Hip Hop as a whole has been influenced by various global dances, but the conversation within the Hip Hop house community points specifically to an ancestral connection with an African dance move that shares its name and essence. The historical African Farmer move, rooted in agricultural life, is celebrated through engaging, ground-oriented movements that mirror the act of sowing seeds—a vital part of African community life.

Hip Hop house dancers, upon observing the similarities and recognizing the cultural significance of this African move, have embraced the name the "Farmer" in an act of respect. This is a reflection of the culture's broader practice of honoring its foundational elements and showing appreciation for the art forms that have shaped its identity.
## Step By Step Instruction

1. Starting Position: Stand with your feet shoulder-width apart, body relaxed and ready to move.

2. The Jump: Hop to your right and lift the left knee simultaneously.

3. The Landing: After the hop, bring your left foot back down while keeping your weight on your right foot.

4. The Switch: Switch your direction to your left and repeat instruction from the jump and now it&#8217;s to the left.

5. Repeat: Continue the sequence, adding in your style, such as arm movements or additional turns, to personalize your Farmer dance.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the Farmer dance?
The Farmer is a hip hop house dance move built on lateral hops paired with opposite-knee lifts — you hop to one side while lifting the opposite knee, then reverse. It's rooted in an African dance of the same name that mimics the motion of sowing seeds, and hip hop house dancers adopted it as a cultural tribute to African dance tradition.

### Where did the Farmer dance come from?
The Farmer's roots are in traditional African dance — specifically a move that depicts agricultural labor as a celebration of community life. Hip hop house dancers in the late 1980s and 1990s recognized the connection and adopted the move, keeping the original name as an act of respect. The move lives in both house dance and pan-African dance traditions today.

### Is the Farmer dance house or hip hop?
Both — it sits squarely in the overlap between house dance and hip hop. It's most commonly taught as a house dance move because it fits house music's 4-on-the-floor rhythm, but it gets used in hip hop freestyle too. The African origins predate both genres, making it a shared cultural inheritance.

### How long does it take to learn the Farmer dance?
Most beginners can get the basic hop-and-knee pattern in 5-10 minutes. Syncing the hop with the opposite knee lift (a classic coordination challenge) takes 20-30 minutes. Making it look grounded and rhythmic instead of jumpy takes a few practice sessions — the move rewards relaxed, connected movement over explosive hops.

### What's the most common Farmer dance mistake?
Lifting the knee too high. The Farmer is a subtle, grounded move — the knee lift should feel natural and relaxed, not like a karate kick. Keep the knee low, let the hop be small and rhythmic, and focus on the connection between the hop and the opposite knee lift. Ground beats air every time.
